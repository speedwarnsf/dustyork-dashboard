Frequently Asked Questions

Testing

How often should I get tested for HIV?

Most people should get tested at least once. If you're sexually active or have other risk factors, the CDC recommends getting tested at least once a year. If you have multiple partners or share injection equipment, testing every 3-6 months is recommended.

Is HIV testing confidential?

Yes. All HIV tests are completely confidential. You can also get anonymous testing at many locations. Healthcare providers are bound by privacy laws to protect your information.

Where can I get tested?

Many places offer HIV testing: health clinics, community health centers, hospitals, urgent care centers, and even at-home tests. Free testing is available at many locations in Cuyahoga County. See our Resources section for local options.

How long does it take to get results?

It depends on the test type. Some rapid tests give results within 20 minutes. Lab-based tests may take a few days. At-home tests typically provide results within 15-20 minutes.
PrEP

What is PrEP?

PrEP (Pre-Exposure Prophylaxis) is a preventive medication for people who don't have HIV but are at risk. When taken as prescribed, PrEP is more than 99% effective at preventing HIV transmission.

Am I a candidate for PrEP?

If you're sexually active and have a partner with HIV, have multiple partners, or share injection equipment, you may be a candidate. Talk to your healthcare provider about whether PrEP is right for you.

How do I access PrEP?

Talk to your healthcare provider or contact one of our resource organizations. Many clinics in Cuyahoga County offer PrEP services. Some programs offer PrEP for free or at reduced cost based on income.

Are there side effects?

Most people tolerate PrEP well. Common side effects are usually mild and temporary, like nausea or headache. Your healthcare provider will monitor you regularly.

Does PrEP protect against other STIs?

PrEP only prevents HIV. You still need to protect yourself against other STIs through safer sex practices, regular testing, and communication with partners.

How often do I need to take PrEP?

There are different options. The most common is daily pills. There's also an injectable form given every 2 months. Your healthcare provider will discuss which option works best for you.

Will I need to take PrEP forever?

No. You can stop taking PrEP anytime. Your healthcare provider will help you decide when it's appropriate to stop based on your risk factors and life circumstances.
U=U (Undetectable = Untransmittable)

What does U=U mean?

Undetectable = Untransmittable means that if you have HIV and are on antiretroviral treatment with an undetectable viral load, you cannot transmit HIV sexually to your partners.

How does treatment make someone undetectable?

HIV treatment (antiretroviral therapy or ART) reduces the viral load in your blood to levels so low that they cannot be detected by standard tests, and more importantly, cannot be transmitted to sexual partners.

How do I know if I'm undetectable?

Your healthcare provider will do a viral load test. You're undetectable when your viral load is below 200 copies/mL (or the laboratory's limit of detection). Regular monitoring ensures you stay undetectable.

Does U=U affect my life?

U=U means you can have a normal life, including relationships without fear of transmitting HIV to partners. It's about living freely and healthily while on treatment.
Syphilis

What is syphilis?

Syphilis is a sexually transmitted bacterial infection. It's easily curable with antibiotics if caught early, but can become serious if left untreated.

What are the signs of syphilis?

Early signs include a painless sore (chancre) or rash. Later symptoms can include fever, swollen lymph nodes, or rashes on palms and soles. Many people have no symptoms.

How is syphilis treated?

Syphilis is easily cured with antibiotics (usually penicillin). Treatment is simple and effective, especially if caught early. See a healthcare provider immediately if you think you may have syphilis.

Why is syphilis testing important?

Many people don't realize they have syphilis because symptoms can be subtle. Regular testing is important if you're sexually active, especially if you have multiple partners.
Stigma

How do I deal with HIV stigma?

Reach out to support groups, counselors, or community organizations. You're not alonemillions of people live with HIV. Many find strength in connecting with others and telling their own story.

What can I do to fight stigma?

Education and open conversation are powerful. Learn the facts about HIV, talk to friends and family, and challenge misunderstandings when you encounter them. Support others living with HIV.

About Love Leads Here

Love Leads Here is a comprehensive HIV and sexual health initiative from Cuyahoga County Board of Health. Our mission is to increase HIV testing, treatment access, and support for people living with or at risk for HIV in Cuyahoga County. We work to combat stigma, provide evidence-based information, and ensure everyone has access to the services they need.

We believe that love leads us to care for ourselves and each other. Whether it's getting tested, taking PrEP, supporting someone living with HIV, or simply treating others with compassion and respect, every action matters. Turn up for your health. Turn up for your community. Turn up for love.
